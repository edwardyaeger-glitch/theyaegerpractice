What We Work On

The concerns that bring people here

Depression

Depression

Persistent low mood, loss of interest, fatigue, hopelessness, or the flattened feeling that nothing quite lands anymore.

Anxiety

Anxiety

Chronic worry, panic, social anxiety, health anxiety, or the low hum of dread that runs underneath an otherwise functional week.

Grief

Grief

Loss of a person, a relationship, a role, a version of yourself. Acute, delayed, or complicated.

Trauma

Trauma

Single-incident or cumulative. Intrusive memories, hypervigilance, numbness, or the sense that part of you is still somewhere else.

ADHD

ADHD

Attention, executive function, time, follow-through. In adults, often diagnosed late and misread for years as character.

Other

Other concerns

Adjustment difficulties, burnout, sleep disruption, obsessive thoughts, or a combination that does not fit neatly into one row. Still worth naming.

These are overlapping, not exclusive. Most people arrive with more than one named challenge.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Do you prescribe medication?
No. I am a licensed mental health counselor, not a prescriber. Clients who benefit from medication are referred to a psychiatrist or primary-care prescriber I coordinate with.
Can I work with you if I am already on medication?
Yes. Many clients are. The therapy work is not a substitute for medication, and it is not in conflict with it.
How long does treatment usually last?
It depends on the concern and the person. Some clients stay for a defined stretch of months; others for years. I do not require a commitment beyond the next session.
Do you do any specific kind of therapy, like CBT or psychodynamic?
My work is integrative. I draw on psychodynamic, relational, cognitive-behavioral, and mindfulness-based approaches depending on what the work calls for. And I psychoeducate accordingly.
Is telehealth as effective as in-person for depression and anxiety?
For most adults, yes. In my experience, the work translates well to video when the presenting concern is outpatient in scope. If a higher level of care is ever indicated, I will say so and help arrange it.
What does a first session actually look like?
A conversation, not a symptom checklist. I ask what is going on, how long it has been going on, what you have already tried, and what you would want to be different. By the end of the session we usually have a shared sense of whether to continue and how often.
How often do we meet?
Weekly is the default. Twice-weekly for a defined stretch is available when clinically indicated, for example during an acute depressive or anxious episode. As things settle, some clients step down to every other week or monthly.
I am in crisis right now. Is this the right place?
If you are in immediate danger, please call or text 988 or go to the nearest emergency room. The practice is an outpatient therapy setting and is not equipped for crisis response between sessions. For clients already established with me, we have a plan for what to do in harder moments.
What if I cannot tell whether I am depressed or just exhausted?
That is a common starting point, and a useful one. Exhaustion, burnout, grief, and depression can look identical from the inside, and they respond to different interventions. Part of the first few sessions is sorting this out together, sometimes in coordination with a physician if sleep, thyroid, or other medical contributors are in play.
How will we know if this is working?
We name what you want to be different at the start, in language specific enough to notice change. That becomes the reference point. Progress in mood work is rarely linear, and I will tell you when I think something is or is not working. If we are six to eight sessions in and nothing is moving, which is rare, we will talk about why and adjust, including the possibility that this is not the right fit.
