Sex, Sexuality, Identity, and Intimate Relationships

Sex is part of life. Therapy should treat it that way.

Psychotherapy for adults working through concerns about sex, sexuality, identity, and intimate relationships.

What We Work On

The concerns that bring people here

Desire

Desire

Mismatched libido, loss of interest, arousal that has changed, or wanting sex that does not look like the script you were handed.

Identity

Identity and orientation

Coming out, being out, questioning, or living an identity that is more complicated than a label. The work that happens before, during, and after.

Intimacy

Intimacy

Closeness, distance, the things that are easy to do and hard to say. Sex is often the place where the rest of a relationship shows up.

Relationship Structure

Relationship structure

Monogamy, non-monogamy, open relationships, polyamory. As practices people already live in, or as questions they are thinking through.

Shame

Shame and history

Religious, cultural, family, or personal histories that still shape the present. What can stay, what most go, and what gets to be added?

Other

Other concerns

Kink, fetish, compulsivity, infidelity, sexual function, sex after trauma. Still worth naming. And even more so worth exploring.

Most of these (and other) concerns overlap. People rarely arrive with only one.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to already have a concern to start working on sex?
No. Some clients arrive with a specific concern. Others come to understand parts of themselves they have never had a private, serious conversation about.
Are you LGBTQIA+ affirming?
Yes. I identify as Queer. I also work through Grey Matter Counseling & Consulting on affirming, trauma-informed work with LGBTQIA+ adults.
Who do you work with?
I work with individuals, couples, polycules, and adult families. Multi-person work is a distinct service and is billed at the corresponding rate on the fees page.
Is what I bring up in session confidential, including sexual content?
Yes. The standard legal and ethical confidentiality rules apply without exception for content related to sexuality.
Do you work with kink, fetish, or non-monogamy?
Yes, without pathologizing them. The work is about what is actually happening for the client, not about whether the practice is mainstream.
Do I have to use clinical language?
No. You can use whatever language you actually use. Part of the work is often finding words that fit, rather than clinical terms that do not. My job is to follow your language, not impose one.
What does a first session look like?
A conversation. I ask what brought you to reach out, what you have and have not been able to talk about elsewhere, and what you would want to be different. Sex, sexuality, and intimacy are treated like any other clinical subjectivity: seriously, and without fuss.
How often do we meet?
Weekly is the default. Couples and polycules sometimes begin at every other week depending on the concern. As the work progresses, cadence is revisited together; it is a clinical decision, not a fixed plan.
What is kept in the record about sexual content?
Progress notes are brief, clinical, and kept in a HIPAA-compliant system. They are not shared with partners, employers, or insurers without written authorization. If a client elects to submit for out-of-network reimbursement, only a superbill, which carries a diagnosis and service code, is disclosed to the insurer.
How do you handle it when a partner is uncomfortable with this work?
Carefully, and without pressure. Discomfort is information, not an obstacle. If one partner is hesitant, we slow down, clarify what the discomfort is about, and decide together what pace and content are workable. Sometimes that means individual sessions for one or both of you before returning to joint work. Sometimes it means the work does not continue, and that is also a legitimate outcome.
