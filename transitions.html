Life Transitions Therapy

You are between one chapter and the next. It should be taken seriously.

Psychotherapy for adults in the middle of a real transition: a divorce, a career pivot, a move, a breakup, a new baby, a loss.

What We Work On

The kinds of transitions I see most often

Partnership

Marriage & committed partnership

Getting ready to merge lives, a rocky first year, a quiet distance a decade in. The work is rarely about a single argument. It is about the shape of two lives built together.

Separation

Divorce & separation

Grief, anger, identity, co-parenting, logistics, decisions you did not plan to make. Therapy gives all of that a place to happen instead of bleeding across the rest of your life.

Work

Career change

Burnout, a layoff, a promotion that feels wrong, a pivot you cannot quite name. I have sat on several sides of the career table and tend to be useful for professionals figuring out the next move.

Place

Relocation

A new city, a return, a move you had to make. Relocation is rarely just logistical. It touches community, identity, and who you get to be next.

Romance

Breakups

A long relationship ending, a short one that hurt more than it should have, or the slow recognition that something was over a while ago. The work: process it, learn from it, get your attention back.

Family

New parenthood

First baby, second baby, blended family. The identity shift is real, and so is the pressure on the partnership. Therapy is a place to name all of it out loud.

Loss

Grief & loss

A person, a role, a version of your life you thought you were going to have. Grief does not move on a schedule. Therapy does not try to rush it.

Other

Other major transitions

Retirement, empty nest, a diagnosis, coming out, estrangement, a sudden financial shift. If it is reshaping your life, it matters.

If none of these fit and you are still in the middle of something that is reshaping your life, know that it is just as significant.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Is a transition always a crisis?
No. Some transitions are chosen; some are imposed. The work is the same either way.
Do I need to know what my transition is before we start?
No. Often the first part of the work is naming what the transition actually is.
How do I know if my situation counts as a transition worth therapy?
If it is reshaping how you spend your days, how you see yourself, or who you are close to, it probably does.
Do you work with people in career pivots or only major life events?
Both. A career pivot is a transition. So is a move, a breakup, a loss, a new role, a new baby, a changed relationship to a parent.
Is telehealth as effective as in-person for transition work?
For most adults working through a transition, yes. In my experience, the work translates well to video when the presenting concern is outpatient in scope. If at any point a higher level of care is indicated, I will say so and help coordinate it.
What does a first session actually look like?
A conversation, not an intake form read aloud. I ask what brought you to reach out now, what you have tried, what has and has not helped, and what you would want to be different. By the end of the session we typically have a working sense of whether to continue and at what cadence.
How often do we meet, and can the cadence change?
Weekly is the default. Twice-weekly for a defined stretch is available when clinically indicated. As the work progresses, some clients step down to every other week or monthly. Cadence is a clinical decision we make together, not a fixed plan.
Who are you not the right fit for?
Clients in active crisis who need stabilization or a higher level of care, clients for whom court-ordered documentation or custody evaluations are the primary goal, and clients seeking medication management without accompanying therapy. In those cases I will refer out.
How do I tell the difference between a transition and burnout?
Burnout tends to be a depletion problem. Rest, boundaries, and a change in workload usually move the needle. A transition is a structural problem. The shape of your life is shifting, and rest alone does not resolve it. The two can coexist, and one can mask the other. Part of early sessions is naming which is which so the work matches the problem.
When does transition work end?
Usually when the new shape of your life feels recognizable as yours, not as something you are still translating. That looks different for everyone. Some clients taper to monthly check-ins. Some finish and return months or years later when the next transition arrives. There is no fixed endpoint, and I do not keep clients longer than we mutually deem the work warrants.
